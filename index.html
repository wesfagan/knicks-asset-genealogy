Prologue

The Trade That Started Everything

January 31, 2019. The Knicks send their best player to Dallas. The basketball world laughs.

On January 31, 2019, the New York Knicks traded Kristaps Porzingis to the Dallas Mavericks. The basketball world laughed — not politely, not behind closed doors, but loudly and publicly, the way people laugh when they've finally seen proof of something they've long suspected. The Knicks were, in fact, as incompetent as everyone believed. Porzingis was 23 years old, a 7'3" unicorn who could shoot threes and protect the rim, the kind of franchise cornerstone that teams spend decades searching for. The Knicks had found him with the fourth pick in 2015, watched him develop into an All-Star, built marketing campaigns around him, and then — just as he was entering his prime — shipped him to Texas for a package that looked like a salary dump rather than a trade.

The return was brutal on paper: Dennis Smith Jr., a point guard who couldn't shoot and was already considered a disappointment two years into his career; DeAndre Jordan, a 30-year-old center on an expiring contract with declining athleticism; Wesley Matthews, another expiring contract who was immediately waived and never played a game for New York; and two first-round picks with protections so convoluted that NBA cap analysts needed spreadsheets to figure out when — or if — they'd ever convey. The Knicks had traded a franchise player for the NBA equivalent of a clearance rack.

Bill Simmons recorded an emergency podcast the night of the trade, gathering his colleagues at The Ringer to try and process what they'd just witnessed. The incredulity was palpable. Former players piled on — Jordan Crawford tweeted "That's all you got for Porzingis??? Hold up man" with a face-palm emoji, speaking for every basketball fan who'd watched the trade scroll across their phone. The tabloids wrote themselves. Every reporter covering the league had the same question: what the hell were the Knicks thinking?

What nobody understood — because the Knicks refused to explain it — was that the trade wasn't really about the players they received. Dennis Smith Jr. was irrelevant. DeAndre Jordan was irrelevant. The whole point was the $70 million in salary cap space the deal created. That summer, Kevin Durant and Kyrie Irving would both be free agents. The plan was simple: clear the books, sign the stars, transform the franchise overnight. The Porzingis trade was a bet on July 2019. Everything the Knicks did that winter — every confusing move, every perplexing decision — was designed to create maximum cap flexibility for the moment when Kevin Durant would finally come home to New York.

The bet failed. Spectacularly. Humiliatingly. In a way that would haunt the organization for years. But what happened next — the strange, winding, improbable path from that failure to the trophy the Knicks lifted in December 2025 — is a story worth telling. Not a story of genius. Not a story of some master plan executed flawlessly. A story of persistence. Of refusing to let dead assets die. Of turning every humiliation into fuel for the next move. Of keeping chains alive when everyone else would have let them break.

The Haul from Dallas

Dennis Smith Jr. — couldn't crack the rotation, averaging 5.5 PPG on 34% shooting before being waived in 2021

DeAndre Jordan — played 19 games, looked washed, salary dumped to Brooklyn

Wesley Matthews — never played a single minute for the Knicks, waived immediately

Two first-round picks — both eventually conveyed, used as pieces in future deals

The players were worthless. The cap space they created was everything.

Chapter One

The Summer Nobody Came

How losing Kevin Durant to Brooklyn forced the Knicks to improvise — and accidentally build a foundation.

The Knicks' pursuit of Kevin Durant had been years in the making — not just months of quiet recruiting, but a fundamental organizational strategy built around one question: how do we position ourselves to sign the best player in the world? Durant was the prize of the 2019 free agent class, the most coveted player to hit the market since LeBron James left Cleveland for Miami in 2010. He was a four-time scoring champion, two-time Finals MVP, and consensus top-three player in the world. He had spent three seasons with the Golden State Warriors, winning two championships, but his relationship with the organization had fractured. Durant bristled at the perception that his titles were somehow diminished by joining an already-dominant team. He wanted a new challenge. He wanted to prove he could win as the unquestioned alpha. He wanted, in short, exactly what the Knicks were selling.

New York seemed like the perfect destination. Durant had grown up in the Washington D.C. area as a Knicks fan, idolizing Patrick Ewing. He had spoken publicly about the allure of Madison Square Garden, calling it "the Mecca of basketball." His business interests were increasingly centered in New York — he had investments in media companies, tech startups, the whole portfolio of a modern athlete building a post-playing career. His agent, Rich Kleiman, was a native New Yorker who desperately wanted to see his client in orange and blue. Even Durant's father, Wayne Pratt, was pushing for the Knicks. He texted his son during free agency with a message that would later leak to reporters: "The Knicks is Mecca. If you want to do it, do it big. If you want to be a New Yorker, be a Knick."

The Knicks pitched Durant aggressively throughout the spring. Team officials met with his representatives multiple times. They prepared elaborate presentations showcasing the franchise's history, the market's opportunities, the chance to be the savior who ended a championship drought that had stretched to 46 years. Owner James Dolan — infamous for meddling, for feuding with fans, for generally making everything worse — was told to stay in the background. The message to Durant was seductive: come to New York, and you'll be bigger than basketball.

What the Knicks didn't see coming was Brooklyn.

The Durant Pitch

The Knicks offered history. The Garden. The chance to be a New York legend.

Rich Kleiman, Durant's agent and a native New Yorker, wanted him to sign with the Knicks. Durant's own father was pushing for Manhattan.

Durant chose Brooklyn anyway.

He'd later request a trade out of there too.

While the Knicks were selling history and market size, the Nets were selling something different: a modern organization run by smart people who would stay out of the way. General manager Sean Marks and coach Kenny Atkinson had taken a franchise that was a laughingstock — worse than the Knicks, if you can believe it, after the disastrous Billy King era — and built it into something respectable. They had a culture. They had young players. They had cap space. And they had a message that resonated with Durant in a way the Knicks' pitch never could.

"I didn't want to be the savior of the Knicks or New York. I didn't care about being the king of New York. That never really moved me. I didn't care about being on Broadway. I just wanted to play ball and go to the crib and chill. And that's what Brooklyn embodied."

— Kevin Durant, interview with Chris Haynes, Yahoo Sports

There it was. The most damning possible rejection. Durant didn't just pick another team — he explained exactly why the Knicks' entire pitch had missed the mark. They had sold him on becoming a savior, and he didn't want to be saved. They had sold him on Broadway, and he wanted to go home and chill. In a later interview, Durant twisted the knife further: "I think a lot of fans look at the Knicks as a brand and expect these younger players in their lifetime don't remember the Knicks being good. The cool thing now is not the Knicks."

On June 30, 2019, Kevin Durant announced he was signing with the Brooklyn Nets. Kyrie Irving joined him. The superteam the Knicks had dreamed of building at Madison Square Garden would instead take shape across the East River, in a building that had hosted the Nets for only seven years. The Knicks were blindsided. Multiple people inside the organization would later describe the atmosphere to The Athletic as one of "shock and despair" — stunned and depressed, like a team that had just lost a playoff series they were supposed to win. They had traded Porzingis for this. They had endured the worst season in franchise history for this. They had $70 million in cap space that was now worthless for its intended purpose.

In the hours after Durant and Irving chose Brooklyn, the Knicks pivoted frantically. Free agency moves fast — the best players sign within hours of midnight on July 1st, and if you're not ready with a backup plan, you're left scrambling. With their primary targets gone, the front office shifted to a new strategy: instead of signing two max players, they would distribute the $70 million across a collection of veterans on short-term deals. Maintain flexibility. Try again later. Hope that something — anything — breaks your way.

The signings came in rapid succession: Bobby Portis (two years, $31 million), Taj Gibson (two years, $20 million), Wayne Ellington, Elfrid Payton, Reggie Bullock. Placeholder contracts designed to preserve future flexibility while putting something resembling an NBA roster on the court. Nobody was excited. The Knicks had swung for superstars and landed role players. The memes wrote themselves. But buried in that collection of placeholder signings were two deals that would prove far more significant than anyone imagined.

The Randle Signing

Julius Randle was 24 years old, a former seventh overall pick who had bounced from the Lakers to the Pelicans without ever establishing himself as more than a decent starter on a bad team. He was talented but deeply flawed — a 6'8" power forward who couldn't shoot from outside, couldn't protect the rim, and had a maddening tendency to play hero ball in crucial moments. His offensive game was built around bullying smaller players in the post, a style that felt increasingly outdated in an NBA that was moving toward spacing and three-point shooting.

Multiple teams had passed on him in free agency. The market was cold. The Knicks offered three years and $63 million — more than anyone else was willing to pay for a player who seemed like a poor fit for modern basketball. At the time, the signing was seen as fine. A decent player on a decent contract. The Knicks had swung for Kevin Durant and landed a tweener power forward with questionable shooting. That was the story.

What nobody predicted was that Randle would completely transform his game over the next two years — developing a three-point shot, becoming a genuine playmaker, turning himself into an All-NBA player. And what nobody could have known was that his transformation would eventually make him valuable enough to trade for Karl-Anthony Towns.

The Morris Signing

Marcus Morris was 29 years old, a journeyman forward who had played for five teams in seven seasons. He could shoot, defend, and provide veteran toughness — exactly the kind of player contenders add in March, not the kind of player rebuilding teams sign in July. He had verbally agreed to a deal with San Antonio before backing out at the last minute and signing with the Knicks for one year and $15 million. The Spurs were furious. The league investigated for tampering. Nothing came of it. Morris headed to New York for what everyone assumed would be a forgettable one-year stint on a bad team.

Instead, Morris played the best basketball of his career. Across 43 games, he averaged 19.6 points on absurd efficiency — 43.9% from three, numbers that made him one of the most valuable trade deadline commodities in the league. The Clippers, desperate for wing depth as they chased a championship, offered a first-round pick. The Knicks accepted.

That pick became the 25th selection in the 2020 draft. The Knicks used it on Immanuel Quickley, a Kentucky guard who nobody was particularly excited about at the time. Quickley would become a fan favorite in New York before eventually being packaged with RJ Barrett and sent to Toronto. The return: OG Anunoby. A one-year rental of a 29-year-old journeyman had become an elite two-way wing locked up through 2029.

Chapter Two

Chaos, Then Silence

David Fizdale gets fired, COVID shuts down the league, and a former agent takes over the franchise.

The 2019-20 season was supposed to be a placeholder — a year to develop young players while the veterans provided stability, a year to wait for the next opportunity. Instead, it became one of the most chaotic seasons in franchise history, which is saying something for an organization that has elevated chaos to an art form. David Fizdale, the head coach, was fired on December 6, 2019, after starting 4-18. His replacement, interim coach Mike Miller, went 17-27 before COVID-19 shut down the league in March. The Knicks finished 21-45 and were not invited to the bubble restart in Orlando — one of only eight teams excluded from the resumed season.

But something important happened nine days before the shutdown: the Knicks hired Leon Rose as president of basketball operations. Rose was an unusual choice — so unusual that it puzzled everyone who covered the league. He had never worked in a front office. Not as a GM, not as an assistant GM, not as a scout, not in any capacity whatsoever. His entire career had been spent as a player agent at Creative Artists Agency, where he had represented some of the biggest names in basketball: LeBron James, Allen Iverson, Carmelo Anthony, Joel Embiid, Karl-Anthony Towns. He was a dealmaker, a relationship builder, someone who understood how the NBA worked from the player's perspective. But building rosters? Evaluating talent? Managing a salary cap? Rose had never done any of it.

The hire was met with skepticism bordering on ridicule. The Knicks, already a punchline, had hired an agent with no front office experience to run basketball operations. It felt like another Dolan special — prioritizing relationships over competence, connections over qualifications. Making matters more complicated, Rose refused to engage with the media. He gave no introductory press conference. He granted no interviews. He made moves in complete silence, offering no explanation for his decisions, no insight into his thinking, no communication of any kind with the fans or reporters who covered the team. In an era of front office transparency — of GMs doing podcasts and explaining their reasoning — Rose operated like a ghost.

What nobody understood at the time was that Rose's silence was a feature, not a bug. He didn't need to explain himself to reporters. He needed to build relationships with players and agents — and in that world, discretion was everything. Rose had spent decades cultivating trust. Players knew that conversations with Leon stayed private. That nothing leaked. That he would protect their interests even when it wasn't convenient. These relationships, built over a lifetime in the agency business, would prove more valuable than any traditional front office credential.

Rose's First Moves

July 2020: Hired Tom Thibodeau as head coach — a defensive mastermind coming off a messy exit from Minnesota where players had revolted against his demanding style.

September 2021: Hired Rick Brunson as assistant coach — Jalen Brunson's father. A nine-month recruiting pitch disguised as a coaching hire.

Rose played chess while everyone else played checkers.

Rose's first major move was hiring Tom Thibodeau as head coach on July 30, 2020. Thibodeau had won Coach of the Year with the Bulls in 2011, transforming a young Chicago team into a defensive juggernaut built around Derrick Rose and Joakim Noah. But his tenure in Minnesota had ended badly — players complaining about his grueling practices, his refusal to play young guys, his obsessive focus on winning at the expense of development. The Timberwolves fired him midway through his third season, and Thibodeau spent the next two years out of the league, waiting for another opportunity.

Rose believed that Thibodeau's intensity was exactly what the Knicks needed. The franchise had been soft for years — lacking accountability, lacking competitive fire, lacking any coherent identity. Thibodeau would demand more. He would make players uncomfortable. He would establish standards that hadn't existed at Madison Square Garden in decades. It was a calculated gamble: the same qualities that had worn out his welcome in Minnesota might be exactly what a dysfunctional organization required.

Chapter Three

The Year Everything Changed

Julius Randle transforms, the defense becomes elite, and Madison Square Garden remembers what playoff basketball feels like.

The Knicks entered the 2020-21 season with modest expectations — and "modest" is being generous. Most projections had them winning somewhere between 25 and 30 games, finishing near the bottom of the Eastern Conference, maybe developing a few young players along the way. The roster was young and unproven: RJ Barrett was entering his second season after a disappointing rookie year in which he shot just 32% from three. Immanuel Quickley was an unknown late first-round pick from Kentucky. Mitchell Robinson was still raw, all athleticism and blocked shots without much offensive polish. The veterans were forgettable — Elfrid Payton starting at point guard, Alec Burks providing bench scoring, Austin Rivers filling out the rotation.

Then Julius Randle transformed.

It's difficult to overstate how dramatic the change was. The player who had been a ball-stopping scorer — someone who caught the ball in the post and tried to bulldoze his way to the rim — suddenly became one of the most versatile power forwards in the league. He developed a three-point shot seemingly overnight, hitting 41.1% on 5.5 attempts per game after shooting 27.7% the previous season. He became a genuine playmaker, running the offense from the elbow, finding cutters, making reads that he'd never shown before. His handle improved. His shot selection improved. His understanding of how to play within Thibodeau's system — how to be aggressive without being selfish, how to create for others while still getting his own — improved beyond anything anyone had predicted.

Randle finished the season averaging 24.1 points, 10.2 rebounds, and 6.0 assists — numbers that would have been impressive for any power forward, let alone one who had been written off as a tweener just two years earlier. He earned his first All-Star selection. He won the Most Improved Player award. He made the All-NBA Second Team. The player the Knicks had signed as a consolation prize after missing out on Kevin Durant had turned himself into a legitimate star.

But Randle's transformation was only part of the story. Under Thibodeau, the Knicks became the fourth-best defensive team in the NBA, allowing just 108.2 points per 100 possessions. They contested every shot. They boxed out on every rebound. They played with a desperation and intensity that the franchise hadn't shown in years — maybe decades. Mitchell Robinson blocked shots. Reggie Bullock chased shooters off the three-point line. Nerlens Noel provided rim protection off the bench. The Knicks were physical, tough, and — for the first time in a long time — genuinely hard to play against.

The Knicks finished 41-31 — their first winning season since 2012-13, their first playoff appearance since 2013, their first moment of genuine relevance in nearly a decade. They earned the fourth seed in the Eastern Conference. Madison Square Garden, finally allowed to welcome fans for the playoffs after COVID restrictions, was electric in a way it hadn't been in years. The crowd chanted "DE-FENSE" like it was 1994, when Patrick Ewing was stalking the paint and John Starks was firing up contested threes. For one series — one brief, beautiful moment — the Knicks felt like the Knicks again.

Then Trae Young happened.

The Hawks' 22-year-old point guard turned the series into a personal showcase, averaging 29.2 points and 9.8 assists across five games. He was unguardable — using floaters, step-backs, and surgical passing to pick apart a Knicks defense that had been elite all season. He torched them from every angle, finding soft spots in their coverage, creating open looks for teammates when they finally committed to stopping him. And most painfully, he did it with a showman's flair, embracing the villain role that the Garden crowd had assigned him. In Game 4, Young hit a game-winning floater and turned to the crowd, blowing a kiss as the arena went silent. The image became iconic — a young star baptizing himself against the NBA's most famous franchise.

Julius Randle, meanwhile, collapsed. The same player who had carried the team all season disappeared when it mattered most, averaging just 18.0 points on 29.8% shooting. He was overwhelmed by the playoff pressure, reverting to the ball-stopping hero ball that had defined him before Thibodeau's arrival. The Knicks lost the series 4-1. The turnaround season ended in disappointment. But the foundation had been laid — and Leon Rose was already planning the moves that would transform disappointment into something else entirely.

Chapter Four

The Brunson Heist

Leon Rose's agency connections, a controversial assistant coaching hire, and the signing that changed everything.

To understand how the Knicks acquired Jalen Brunson, you have to understand the web of relationships that Leon Rose had spent decades cultivating — and the lengths he was willing to go to leverage them. Brunson was a second-round pick in 2018, selected 33rd overall by Dallas. He was considered undersized at 6'1", not athletic enough to be a star, limited in the ways that prevent guards from becoming more than role players. But he was a winner in ways that the draft process struggled to quantify — consensus National Player of the Year at Villanova, where he'd led the Wildcats to two national championships in 2016 and 2018. Dallas saw him as a backup point guard, someone who could run the second unit while Luka Dončić carried the offense.

By the 2022 playoffs — his contract year — Brunson had exploded beyond anyone's expectations. He averaged 21.6 points in the postseason, leading Dallas to the Western Conference Finals while playing with a confidence and poise that suggested he was ready for a much larger role. In the first round against Utah, he averaged 27.8 points while Dončić dealt with a calf injury, carrying the Mavericks almost single-handedly. He was no longer a backup. He was a legitimate star — and he was about to become a free agent.

Here's where the relationships mattered. Brunson was represented by CAA — Leon Rose's former agency, the same company where he'd spent his entire career before joining the Knicks. His primary agent was Sam Rose, Leon's son. The family connection was direct and undeniable. And in September 2021, the Knicks had hired Rick Brunson — Jalen's father — as an assistant coach under Thibodeau. The implications were obvious. The league investigated for tampering. The Knicks were eventually fined a second-round pick — a slap on the wrist that infuriated Dallas owner Mark Cuban.

The Cap Space Math

The Knicks needed ~$30M in space to sign Brunson. They had ~$18M available.

June 23, 2022: Traded Kemba Walker, Nerlens Noel, and Alec Burks to Detroit — attaching two second-round picks and $6M in cash just to get teams to take the contracts.

They literally paid to give away players.

One week later: Brunson signed for 4 years, $104M.

"Where it went south was when Rick took over, when the parent took over, or parents took over."

— Mark Cuban, explaining why Dallas couldn't re-sign Brunson, June 2023

Cuban was pissed — and he had reason to be. The Knicks had essentially recruited Brunson for nine months while he was still under contract with Dallas, using his father's coaching position as cover for what was obviously a long-term pursuit. When Cuban was asked about the tampering fine, he didn't hide his frustration: "Was I happy that they only got dinged for a second-round pick? No. No, it should've been far worse. But it is what it is."

The Brunson contract was immediately controversial. ESPN's Brian Windhorst called it "a lot of money for a guy who's never been the man." Anonymous executives told The Athletic the Knicks had "massively overpaid" for a player who had never been a full-time starter. Dallas, who could have matched the offer and kept Brunson, chose not to — they didn't believe he was worth max money, and they had concerns about paying a small guard big money when Dončić needed the ball in his hands.

In his first season with the Knicks, Brunson averaged 24.0 points and 6.2 assists, making his first All-Star team and instantly becoming the best point guard the franchise had employed since... when? Mark Jackson? The "overpay" looked reasonable. In his second season, he averaged 28.7 points and finished sixth in MVP voting, carrying the Knicks to the second seed in the East. The contract became one of the best values in the league — a franchise point guard locked up for four years at roughly $26 million annually while comparable players were signing for $50 million or more.

Cuban eventually admitted the obvious: letting Brunson walk was a mistake. The Mavericks had a future All-Star on their roster and let him leave for nothing because they didn't want to pay him. It was exactly the kind of error that Leon Rose had spent his entire career helping players avoid — and exactly the kind of opportunity he had positioned the Knicks to exploit.

Chapter Five

The Bust Chain

How Kevin Knox — the worst draft pick of the rebuild — eventually became Josh Hart.

Kevin Knox II was selected ninth overall in the 2018 draft, the same draft that produced Luka Dončić, Trae Young, and Shai Gilgeous-Alexander. He was 18 years old, a 6'9" forward from Kentucky with a smooth shooting stroke and tantalizing upside. The selection was considered reasonable at the time — Knox was young, skilled, and had the kind of physical profile that NBA teams dream on. He was supposed to be a building block, a piece of the Knicks' future alongside Porzingis.

The pick was a disaster. Knox shot 37.0% from the field as a rookie — one of the worst marks for any lottery pick in recent memory. His three-point shot, supposedly his calling card, landed at just 34.3%. He didn't defend. He didn't rebound. He didn't do anything that suggested he belonged on an NBA court. By his second season, his shooting percentage dropped to 36.8%. By his third season, he was a ghost: 12.4 minutes per game in 42 sporadic appearances, buried at the end of Tom Thibodeau's bench, rarely seeing the court in meaningful situations. The Knicks had used the ninth pick on a player who couldn't crack the rotation of a team that won 41 games.

But Knox was still on a rookie contract — cheap and tradeable. In January 2022, the Knicks sent him to Atlanta for Cam Reddish, another lottery disappointment. Reddish had been the tenth pick in 2019, a player with elite physical tools who had never figured out how to use them consistently. The trade was a swap of busts, two teams hoping that a change of scenery might unlock something. It didn't. By the 2022-23 season, Thibodeau had removed Reddish from the rotation entirely. He hadn't played since December.

In February 2023, the Knicks flipped Reddish to Portland for Josh Hart. The trade happened while Jalen Brunson was at Villanova, watching his jersey get retired alongside Hart's. When someone showed Brunson the news on their phone, he threw his arms up and screamed "Oh shit!" — the reaction captured on video and immediately shared across social media. Hart was exactly what the Knicks needed: a 6'4" guard/forward who could defend multiple positions, crash the offensive glass with reckless abandon, and make all the winning plays that don't show up in box scores.

Chapter Six

The Five-Pick Gamble

Leon Rose bets the franchise's draft future on another Villanova reunion.

By the summer of 2024, the Knicks had assembled the pieces of a contender — but they weren't yet a team that could realistically win a championship. Brunson was the offensive engine, one of the best scorers in the league. OG Anunoby, acquired from Toronto in December 2023, was the lockdown defender they'd lacked. Hart provided energy and toughness. But they needed one more piece — a third star who could take defensive pressure off Brunson and create offense when the playoffs slowed everything down.

Mikal Bridges was available. He was 27 years old, entering his seventh NBA season. He had been drafted by Philadelphia, traded to Phoenix on draft night, and spent five seasons as a starter on competitive Suns teams that had reached the Finals in 2021. He was one of the most durable players in the league — 470 consecutive games entering 2023-24 — and one of the best perimeter defenders. He shot 37.5% from three, rarely turned the ball over, and made winning plays on both ends. In 2023, he had been traded to Brooklyn as part of the Kevin Durant package, and now the rebuilding Nets were willing to move him for the right price.

That price was astronomical: four unprotected first-round picks (2025, 2027, 2029, 2031), one protected first-round pick via Milwaukee, an unprotected pick swap in 2028, and Bojan Bogdanovic's salary. The haul was the largest draft capital package ever traded for a player who had never been an All-Star. NBA executives called it insane. They said Brooklyn had fleeced the Knicks. They predicted it would haunt the franchise for a decade if Bridges didn't work out.

Leon Rose, characteristically, said almost nothing. When pressed by reporters about the risk, he offered one of his rare public comments: "We're not rebuilding. We're competing." That was it. No elaboration, no explanation, no defense of the price. Just a simple statement of intent: the Knicks were going all-in, and if it didn't work, they would deal with the consequences later.

On July 2, 2024, Bridges signed a four-year, $150 million extension with the Knicks. The Nova Knicks — Brunson, Hart, Bridges, and the soon-to-be-traded Donte DiVincenzo, all from Villanova's championship teams — were finally reunited professionally for the first time since college. The basketball world watched with a mixture of fascination and skepticism. Could you really build a championship team around four players from the same college program? Did the chemistry of 2016 and 2018 translate to the NBA? Was this genius or nostalgia masquerading as strategy?

On September 28, 2024, the Knicks traded Julius Randle and Donte DiVincenzo to Minnesota for Karl-Anthony Towns. The final piece was in place.

The consolation prize from losing Kevin Durant became a franchise center on a max contract.

The Towns trade was stunning in its symmetry. Both teams had problems that the other could solve — Minnesota needed to get under the luxury tax while adding a player who fit better alongside Anthony Edwards; New York needed a center who could shoot and space the floor alongside Brunson. Towns was 28 years old, a former number one pick who had been an All-Star multiple times but had never won a playoff series. He was one of the best shooting big men in NBA history — career 41.3% from three, absurd numbers for someone his size — but questions lingered about his defense, his toughness, his ability to win games that mattered.

Randle, meanwhile, had become an awkward fit in New York. His usage overlapped with Brunson's in ways that limited both players. His struggles in the playoffs — disappearing in crucial moments, reverting to hero ball under pressure — had raised questions about his ceiling. And his relationship with the fan base had soured after he gave a thumbs-down gesture to the Garden crowd during a frustrating loss. The Towns trade allowed the Knicks to move on while getting a player who better complemented their core.

The reaction was predictably mixed. Kendrick Perkins, never one for understatement, called it "arguably the worst trade in NBA history" for Minnesota. Zach Lowe disagreed, arguing that Randle was "probably better than Towns this year at almost half the price." Towns himself was shocked — he had expected to spend his entire career in Minnesota and was blindsided when Tim Connelly showed up at his house to deliver the news in person. "I'm still stunned," Towns said months later. "It's weird. I feel more like a Knick now after everything we went through last year, but it's weird to see that Wolves jersey and not see 'Towns' on the back of it."

Chapter Seven

The Trophy

December 2025. Las Vegas. The first Knicks hardware in fifty-two years.

On December 16, 2025, the New York Knicks defeated the San Antonio Spurs 124-113 in Las Vegas to win the NBA Cup. Let's be clear about what this is: the NBA Cup is not the championship. It's a mid-season tournament, introduced in 2023, designed to add stakes to November and December games that fans had historically ignored. The prize money is nice — $500,000 per player on the winning team. The trophy is real. But it's not the Larry O'Brien. It's not what Knicks fans have been waiting for since 1973.

And yet. For a franchise that had won nothing of significance in fifty-two years — no championships, no conference titles, no division banners worth celebrating — even this mattered. The NBA Cup was the first Knicks trophy since Willis Reed and Walt Frazier were still playing, the first time anyone associated with the organization had lifted hardware since before most current fans were born. It wasn't everything. But it was something.

The Spurs had the Knicks down 11 points with just over two minutes left in the third quarter. Victor Wembanyama was dominant, looking every bit like the generational prospect the league had hyped him to be. The game felt like it was slipping away. Then the Knicks' reserves took over: Mitchell Robinson grabbed 15 rebounds — including 10 offensive — in 18 minutes, his length and energy disrupting everything San Antonio tried to do inside. Tyler Kolek, a rookie point guard most casual fans had never heard of, scored 14 points with five rebounds and five assists. Jordan Clarkson added 15 points off the bench. The Knicks outscored the Spurs by 19 in the fourth quarter, turning a likely loss into a comfortable win.

Jalen Brunson was named tournament MVP, averaging 33.2 points per game across the Cup bracket. In the championship game, he finished with 25 points and eight assists, controlling the fourth quarter when it mattered most. The T-Mobile Arena crowd — heavily pro-Knicks, because Knicks fans travel and there are millions of them scattered across the country — chanted "MVP" every time Brunson touched the ball.

Brunson credited his teammates — Anunoby, Robinson, Kolek, Clarkson — and deflected the individual praise. "Without them, we don't win this," he said on the podium. It was the right thing to say, the team-first rhetoric that champions always offer. But there was something genuine in it too: the Knicks had won this tournament not because one player dominated, but because the roster was deep enough to survive a third-quarter collapse and talented enough to flip the game when it mattered.

The confetti fell. The trophy was lifted. Players who had been assembled from trades and free agency — from consolation prizes and salary dumps and busted draft picks — stood together on a stage in Las Vegas. It wasn't Madison Square Garden. It wasn't a championship. But it was proof that the rebuild had produced something tangible, that the chains Leon Rose had spent years cultivating had finally connected into something real.

Chapter Eight

The Window

What comes next — and why the next four months matter more than anything the franchise has done in decades.

The Knicks didn't tank. They didn't land the first overall pick. They didn't sign Kevin Durant or Kyrie Irving or any of the superstars who were supposed to save the franchise. They failed at everything they tried — the Porzingis era ended in acrimony, the Durant pitch ended in humiliation, the Knox pick ended in embarrassment, the 2021 playoff run ended with Trae Young blowing kisses at the Garden crowd.

And yet.

The Porzingis trade created cap space that signed Randle and Morris. Randle transformed into an All-Star, then became the centerpiece of a trade for Karl-Anthony Towns. Morris was flipped for a first-round pick that became Quickley, who was packaged with RJ Barrett for OG Anunoby. The Knox bust became a trade chip that eventually yielded Josh Hart. The salary cap gymnastics required to sign Brunson produced the franchise's best player since Patrick Ewing. Every failure led somewhere. Every dead end opened a new path.

There's an argument that Leon Rose got lucky. That Marcus Morris playing well was random. That Randle's transformation was unpredictable. That Immanuel Quickley developing into a tradeable asset was a happy accident. That the whole thing could have gone differently with a few bounces, a few injuries, a few players who didn't develop the way they did.

Maybe. Luck is always part of it. But luck favors the prepared — and the Knicks, for once, were prepared. They didn't panic when Durant chose Brooklyn. They didn't blow up the roster when Randle struggled in the playoffs. They didn't force trades just to make moves. They kept making calculated bets, kept turning assets over, kept believing that persistence would eventually pay off. In December 2025, they lifted a trophy. The first Knicks hardware in 52 years.

The Outlook

Championship odds: +1400 (4th in NBA)

Eastern Conference: Wide open — Tatum (Celtics) and Haliburton (Pacers) both out with Achilles injuries

Path to Finals: As clear as it's been since 1999

Concerns: Brunson-Towns defensive pairing remains questionable; depth unproven in playoff settings

"Title or bust" — Basketball News season preview headline

The question now is whether they can lift another one — the Larry O'Brien Trophy, the real one, the one that's eluded Madison Square Garden since Willis Reed limped onto the court in 1970 and Patrick Ewing fell short in 1994 and every team since has failed to capture. The path has never been clearer. The Celtics are dealing with a gap year after Jayson Tatum's Achilles injury. The Pacers lost Tyrese Haliburton to the same cruel fate. Philadelphia continues to juggle health concerns around Joel Embiid. The Eastern Conference is there for the taking — a rare alignment where the Knicks' window coincides with their competitors' misfortune.

But there are no guarantees. The Knicks' fatal flaw remains their defensive pairing of Brunson and Towns — neither is a stopper, and together they submitted middling defensive numbers last season. The bench, while improved by the Clarkson signing, remains unproven in playoff settings where rotations shorten and weaknesses get exposed. And the pressure of expectation — the weight of 52 years, the scrutiny of the New York market, the knowledge that this is the window and there may not be another — could crush a team that's never won anything meaningful together.

Fifty-two years is a long time. But every drought ends eventually. The Knicks have the talent. They have the depth. They have a point guard who plays his best basketball in the biggest moments and a roster constructed specifically to complement his strengths. They've already proven they can win when it matters — the NBA Cup showed that. What remains to be seen is whether they can do it again, four more times, against the best teams in the world, when everything is on the line.

Maybe this is the year.
