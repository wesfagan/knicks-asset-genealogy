What Actually Happened

New York Knicks, 2017–2025

The popular story is that Leon Rose masterfully acquired a championship core through clever trades. The actual story is stranger: the Knicks kept failing upward. Every asset that worked out was a consolation prize for something that didn't. The roster exists because the front office kept the chain alive long enough for accidents to compound.

But here's the thing about chains: they don't stop. The players we have now aren't endpoints. They're current positions in a system that keeps moving. And some of those positions might be more fragile than they look.

· · ·

I. The Porzingis Trade Was Not a Heist

In January 2019, Kristaps Porzingis demanded a trade. He was 23, 7'3", and had just recovered from a torn ACL. The Knicks had no leverage.

Dallas offered Dennis Smith Jr. (a failed lottery pick), two expiring contracts (Wesley Matthews, DeAndre Jordan), and two first-round picks with protections. The Knicks said yes.

At the time, this was considered a loss. DSJ was struggling. The picks had murky protections. The expirings were, well, expiring.

But here's what matters: those expirings created $70 million in cap space for summer 2019. And that cap space — not the picks, not DSJ — is where everything came from.

The Knicks wanted Kevin Durant and Kyrie Irving. They got Julius Randle and Marcus Morris.

This is the origin of everything.

The Uncomfortable Part

The Knicks whiffed on Durant and Kyrie. They signed Randle and Morris as consolation. Those two consolation prizes — a prove-it deal and a one-year rental — are the entire foundation of the current roster. The "genius moves" were all downstream of a failure.

II. The Morris Flip Is Underrated

Marcus Morris signed a 1-year, $15 million deal in July 2019. He was 29, a journeyman, a rental. Nobody thought about him.

Then he played the best basketball of his life. 19.6 points per game. 44% from three. He became a trade deadline asset.

In February 2020, the Clippers needed forward depth. They traded a first-round pick for Morris. That pick became #25 in the November 2020 draft.

The Knicks selected Immanuel Quickley.

Quickley became a fan favorite, a Sixth Man candidate, a spark plug. But here's what the Knicks understood that the fans didn't: Quickley's value was as a trade chip, not as a long-term piece. They declined to extend him.

In December 2023, Quickley (plus RJ Barrett and a second) became OG Anunoby.

A one-year rental of a 29-year-old journeyman became an elite 3-and-D wing on a 5-year, $212 million extension.

III. The Bust That Kept Giving

Kevin Knox was the #9 pick in 2018. He was supposed to be a scorer. He couldn't score. He couldn't defend. He couldn't stay in the rotation.

By 2021, Knox was worthless. Except he wasn't — he was a draft asset who still had theoretical upside. Atlanta took a chance.

In January 2022, Knox (plus a protected Charlotte first) became Cam Reddish. Reddish was another former lottery pick who wasn't working out. The Knicks figured they'd try to unlock him.

They couldn't. Reddish played 35 games. He was out of the rotation by spring.

But again: not worthless. In February 2023, Reddish (plus a lottery-protected first) became Josh Hart.

Hart is now on a 4-year, $81 million extension. He's a starter. He's beloved. He's the emotional center of the team.

A #9 bust → a #10 bust → a core player.

The Knicks didn't draft well. They just kept the chain alive.

IV. The Brunson Signing Required Sacrifice

In 2020, the Knicks signed Nerlens Noel and Alec Burks. Solid players. Combined $62 million in extensions.

Two years later, both were dumped to Detroit. The Knicks attached two second-round picks and $6 million in cash just to get rid of them.

This created approximately $26 million in cap space.

That space was used to sign Jalen Brunson outright as a free agent, avoiding the need for a sign-and-trade with Dallas.

Brunson is now on a 4-year, $156 million extension — and he reportedly left $113 million on the table to help the team's flexibility.

Noel and Burks weren't failures. They were fuel.

What the Salary Cap Actually Is

The salary cap isn't a limit. It's a resource. Every contract is potential energy. The Knicks understood that bad contracts aren't dead — they're dormant trade chips waiting to match salaries. Fournier's $73 million disaster became the ballast that got them Bridges. Noel and Burks became Brunson. Nothing is wasted if you keep it moving.

V. The Paths That Died

Not everything worked. Some chains ended.

Dennis Smith Jr. was the "centerpiece" of the Porzingis trade. He became Derrick Rose, who left in free agency. That path is gone.

Obi Toppin was the #8 pick in 2020. MSG chanted his name for years. He was traded to Indiana for two distant second-round picks. Gone.

RJ Barrett was the #3 pick in 2019. Extended for 4 years, $107 million. Traded to Toronto as part of the OG deal. The pick became trade fodder.

Isaiah Hartenstein signed for $16 million over two years, played brilliantly, then left for OKC's $87 million offer. The Knicks had only Early Bird rights. They couldn't match. Gone.

These aren't failures in the traditional sense. They're paths that didn't compound. The difference between Knox and Toppin isn't that Knox was better — it's that Knox's chain led somewhere. Toppin's didn't.

The Final Accounting

Jalen Brunson
4yr / $156M extension
Origin: Noel and Burks, signed in 2020, dumped in 2022 with picks and cash attached to create cap space. The Knicks paid to erase useful players just to sign Brunson outright.
The load question: 6'2", 30+ usage rate, carrying everything. His body has held up. So far.
OG Anunoby
5yr / $212M extension
Origin: Marcus Morris, a prove-it signing after the Durant/Kyrie whiff, flipped for a 1st that became Quickley, who was traded with RJ Barrett for OG. Six years, four players, one outcome.
The health question: Chronic hamstring issues. Has never played 70 games in a season. Elite when available. When.
Karl-Anthony Towns
4yr / $220M (via MIN)
Origin: Julius Randle, signed with the same cap space that signed Morris. Extended for $117M in 2022. That extension made his salary matchable for Towns. The "failed" 2019 free agency still paying dividends in 2024.
The fit question: Elite offensive talent. Finally broke through in the 2025 playoffs. But at $55M/year, the margin for error is zero.
Mikal Bridges
4yr / $150M extension
Origin: Dallas picks from KP trade → Grimes → Bogdanovic (salary ballast, held 4 months) → Bridges. Also required Fournier's bloated contract as outgoing salary. The mistake became leverage.
The cost question: Five first-round picks. Five. What if this version of Bridges is the version? What if it plateaus here?
Josh Hart
4yr / $81M extension
Origin: Kevin Knox, the #9 bust → Cam Reddish, the #10 bust → Josh Hart. Two lottery failures, flipped within 14 months, became a core player. The chain never broke.
The role question: Does everything. At 29. How long can "does everything" last at his size and intensity?
Mitchell Robinson
4yr / $60M extension
Origin: Carmelo Anthony, traded to OKC in 2017 for Enes Kanter, Doug McDermott, and a throwaway 2nd round pick. That pick became Robinson. Eight years ago.
The availability question: Elite rim protector. Can't stay on the floor. 65 games missed last year alone.

The Point

There was no master plan. There was no genius. There was a franchise that kept making moves, kept the chains alive, kept turning failures into fuel and mistakes into leverage. The roster exists not because of what went right, but because of what kept moving.

That's the actual story. That's the structure underneath the narrative.

VI. But Now What?

The story so far has been about how we got here. But "here" is not a destination. It's a position. And positions change.

The Knicks traded five first-round picks for Mikal Bridges. Five. That's 2025, 2027, 2029, 2031, plus swap rights in 2028. That's the next decade of draft capital, sent to Brooklyn.

This is not asset management. This is conviction. The front office is saying: we believe this core is it, and we believe the window is now.

Maybe they're right. Maybe they're early. Maybe they're late.

But here's what's certain: the optionality is gone. The chains that led here have been converted into contracts and commitments. There are no more "let's see how it develops" paths left. This is the team.

And the team made the conference semifinals in 2025. It's working. The question is whether it can work all the way.

The Real Question

If the Knicks knew in 2019 that missing Durant and Kyrie would eventually lead to Brunson, OG, KAT, Bridges, and Hart — they'd be thrilled. That's a core. That's real. They made the conference semifinals in 2025. This isn't theoretical anymore.

The question isn't whether the asset work was good. It was. The question is what comes next: Can this core take the final step? And how long do they have to figure it out?

The chains are locked in now. The flexibility has been converted into talent. What's left is execution.

VII. The Fragility Beneath

Every roster has fragilities. The Knicks' fragilities are specific:

Brunson's size. He's 6'2" in a league that keeps getting longer. His game is predicated on craft, not explosion. He's 28. How many more years at this usage rate before something gives?

OG's hamstrings. He's never played 70 games. Not once. Elite defender. Elite three-point shooter. Chronically unavailable. They gave him $212 million betting the pattern breaks.

KAT's price tag. The most expensive player in franchise history. He broke through in the 2025 playoffs — finally won series after years of Minnesota heartbreak. But at $55M annually, he needs to be transcendent, not just good. The ceiling is All-NBA. The floor is an unmovable contract.

Bridges' ceiling. In Phoenix, he was a perfect third option. In Brooklyn, he was a volume scorer on a bad team. In New York, he's supposed to be... what exactly? The numbers in his first Knicks year suggest "good starter" — not "five first-round picks."

Robinson's body. Elite when healthy. Rarely healthy. At some point, the Knicks have to ask: is he the center, or is he the idea of a center?

None of these are guarantees of failure. But they're not nothing, either. They're structural risks — and the Knicks have bet big that they won't compound into something worse.

A Note on Windows

Championship windows are strange. You never know you're in one until you're out of one. And by then, it's too late to go back.

The Knicks believe they're opening a window. The moves suggest urgency: the Bridges trade, the Towns trade, the extensions, the willingness to pay luxury tax. This is a franchise that has decided the time is now.

But "now" is a tricky word. It assumes health. It assumes chemistry. It assumes that the pieces fit the way you imagined. It assumes the league doesn't evolve faster than your roster can adapt.

What if Brunson's body doesn't hold up to the workload?

What if OG misses 25 games again?

What if Bridges' role never clarifies?

What if KAT's playoff breakthrough was the ceiling, not the floor?

What if the coaching change takes longer to metabolize than expected?

These aren't predictions. They're possibilities. And the problem with betting big is that you're exposed to all of them at once.

The chains got us here. But the chains don't guarantee anything about what comes next. That's the part no one likes to say out loud.

The Math

By 2028, the core will be in their early 30s. The first-round picks are gone until 2033. The payroll is locked.

But that's the point. The Knicks didn't build this roster to have options. They built it to win. They traded future flexibility for present talent. That's not reckless — that's conviction.

Every championship team eventually faces a reckoning. The question is whether they get the ring first. The Knicks bet yes. Time will tell if they were right.

One More Thing

Tom Thibodeau is gone. Mike Brown is in.

Thibs built this roster. His fingerprints are on everything: the defensive identity, the grinding pace, the willingness to play starters 40 minutes. Brown inherits all of it — the players, the contracts, the expectations — but not the relationships or the system.

Coaching changes mid-window are risky. They disrupt chemistry. They force players to relearn roles. They introduce uncertainty at exactly the moment you need certainty.

Maybe Brown unlocks something Thibs couldn't. Maybe he simplifies the offense, manages minutes better, gets more from the bench. Or maybe it takes a year — a year they might not have — to figure it out.

The asset story doesn't account for this. The chains don't care about coaching changes or locker room dynamics or whether Brunson and KAT have figured out how to play together. They just show what was traded and what was received.

But basketball isn't accounting. The ledger is only part of the story.

The Weight of the City

There's something the asset chains don't capture: the psychological weight of being the Knicks.

Fifty-two years since the last championship. Ewing never won. Melo never got past the second round. KP demanded out. The Garden is both a cathedral and a pressure cooker.

And now this core — Brunson, KAT, OG, Bridges, Hart — is supposed to be the one that finally does it. Brunson's dad played here. The city is watching. The expectations are enormous.

The chains got them here. But the chains can't carry that weight. Only the players can.

If We're Being Honest

The Knicks have done something remarkable. They took a series of accidents, failures, and consolation prizes and turned them into a conference semifinals team with legitimate championship aspirations.

The asset work is done. The roster is built. What happens next is about basketball, not transactions.

The chains led here. Now the players have to finish the story.

One Last Thing

Eight years of trades, signings, and draft picks. Lines connecting assets to assets. Some paths dead-ended. Others kept forking and recombining until they arrived at this moment.

And at the end of all those lines: five players in Knicks jerseys. Brunson, KAT, OG, Bridges, Hart. A conference semifinals team. A legitimate contender.

The chains tell you everything about how we got here. They tell you nothing about what happens next. That part is up to the players.

The bets are placed. The roster is built. Now we find out what it was all for.
